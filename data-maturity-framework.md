# NASA Data Maturity Assessment Framework

## Overview

The NASA Data Maturity Assessment (DMA) Framework is an enterprise-wide governance benchmark designed to evaluate how effectively NASA organizational units manage, govern, and leverage data and AI systems in support of the agency's mission.

The framework was developed through a collaborative process involving the Chief Data Officer (CDO) and Chief Artificial Intelligence Officer (CAIO) teams at NASA Headquarters, with input from stakeholders across NASA centers. It draws on established federal and international governance standards to ensure alignment with broader policy requirements.

---

## Design Philosophy

The framework is built around a core principle: governance is not a compliance exercise, it is operational infrastructure. A maturity assessment is only meaningful if it produces actionable insights that organizations can use to improve, not just a score that sits in a report.

With that in mind, the DMA was designed to be:

- **Consistent** so that the same assessment methodology applies across all NASA centers, enabling meaningful cross-organizational comparison
- **Contextually meaningful** so that maturity levels are defined in ways that account for the diversity of NASA's operational contexts, from research laboratories to mission operations
- **Actionable** so that assessment outputs translate directly into governance priorities and improvement roadmaps
- **Scalable** so that the technical infrastructure supports agency-wide deployment without requiring significant local resources

---

## Framework Pillars

The NASA DMA Framework evaluates organizational maturity across six interconnected pillars. Together they capture the full spectrum of what it means for a federal agency to govern data responsibly and effectively.

### 1. Data Governance and Risk Management
This pillar explores how the agency defines roles, responsibilities, and policies to ensure data is managed responsibly, securely, and in compliance with regulations. It focuses on how risks are identified and mitigated to protect data integrity and trust.

### 2. Data Architecture and Systems Infrastructure
This pillar examines the technical foundation that supports NASA's data environment, including how systems are designed, integrated, and modernized to enable secure, scalable, and interoperable data use across the organization.

### 3. Data Lifecycle and Quality Management
This pillar focuses on how the agency manages data throughout its lifecycle, from creation and maintenance to archival and retirement. It emphasizes practices that ensure data remains accurate, accessible, and fit for purpose.

### 4. Analytics, AI and Decision Intelligence
This pillar assesses how the agency transforms data into actionable insights using analytics, artificial intelligence, and decision support tools. It highlights the responsible use of AI to drive smarter, faster, and more informed decisions.

### 5. Data Protection, Privacy and Compliance
This pillar evaluates how the agency safeguards data through security protocols, privacy practices, and compliance with legal and ethical standards. It emphasizes protecting sensitive information and maintaining public trust.

### 6. Workforce, Culture and Data Literacy
This pillar looks at how the agency builds a data-driven culture through training, staffing, and leadership. It focuses on empowering employees with the skills and mindset needed to use data effectively and responsibly.

## Maturity Scale

The DMA uses a tiered maturity scale that moves organizations from ad hoc, uncoordinated practices toward systematic, optimized governance. The scale is designed to be descriptive rather than purely prescriptive, recognizing that different organizational units may have legitimate reasons for operating at different maturity levels depending on their mission context.

## Policy Foundations

The framework draws on the following governance standards and references:

| Standard | Relevance |
|----------|-----------|
| NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) | AI safety, accountability, and risk management |
| OECD Recommendation on Artificial Intelligence | International AI governance alignment |
| IAPP AI Governance in Practice Report | Organizational implementation practices |
| Foundations for Evidence-Based Policymaking Act | Federal data governance compliance |
| NASA internal data policies and standards | Agency-specific requirements |

## Key Design Challenges

Building a governance benchmarking tool for an agency as large and diverse as NASA surfaced several design challenges that have broader implications for federal AI and data governance.

**Standardization vs. context-sensitivity.** A framework that works for a research laboratory may not translate directly to a mission operations team. Designing assessment criteria that are consistent enough to enable comparison while remaining meaningful across very different organizational contexts required significant iteration.

**Translating policy into practice.** High-level governance frameworks like the NIST AI RMF articulate important principles, but they do not prescribe operational systems. Determining how to operationalize concepts like "accountability" or "transparency" in a way that could be measured and scored required both policy research and technical implementation judgment.

**Varying maturity baselines.** NASA centers enter the assessment with vastly different levels of existing data governance infrastructure. The framework had to be calibrated to produce useful diagnostics for both highly mature and early-stage organizations.

**Stakeholder alignment.** Governance tools are only effective if the people they are designed to serve understand and trust them. Building buy-in across diverse stakeholders with different priorities and levels of data literacy was as important as the technical design itself.
