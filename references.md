# Key References and Resources

This document lists the primary frameworks, standards, and resources that informed the development of the NASA Data Maturity Assessment Framework and the policy research contained in this repository.

---

## AI Governance Frameworks

**NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)**
U.S. Department of Commerce, National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023
https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.100-1

The AI RMF provides a voluntary framework for organizations to manage risks associated with AI systems across their full lifecycle. It was a foundational reference for the DMA's AI safety and governance pillars.

---

**OECD Recommendation of the Council on Artificial Intelligence**
Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, 2024
https://legalinstruments.oecd.org/en/instruments/OECD-LEGAL-0449

The OECD AI Recommendation establishes international principles for trustworthy AI, covering transparency, accountability, robustness, and human oversight. It informed the DMA's alignment with international governance standards.

---

**AI Governance in Practice Report 2024**
International Association of Privacy Professionals (IAPP), 2024
https://iapp.org/resources/article/ai-governance-in-practice-report

This report examines how organizations across sectors are operationalizing AI governance beyond compliance checkboxes. It provided practical benchmarking context for the DMA's design.

---

## Federal Data Governance

**Foundations for Evidence-Based Policymaking Act of 2018**
U.S. Congress, 2018

Establishes requirements for federal agencies to develop and implement data governance plans, ensuring data is managed as a strategic asset.

---

**Federal Data Strategy**
U.S. Office of Management and Budget

Provides a common set of principles and best practices for federal agencies to leverage data as a strategic asset.

---

## Biosecurity and AI

**Contemporary Foundation AI Models Increase Biological Weapons Risk**
Roger Brent and T. Greg McKelvey, Jr.
RAND Global and Emerging Risks, Working Paper WR-A3853-1, June 2025
https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.13798

This working paper examines how already-deployed foundation AI models can guide motivated users through the technical processes required for biological weapons development, challenging assumptions about tacit knowledge as a barrier.

## Further Reading

**The Code Breaker: Jennifer Doudna, Gene Editing, and the Future of the Human Race**
Walter Isaacson, Simon & Schuster, 2021

An exploration of how CRISPR and gene editing technologies have democratized access to biological research, with significant implications for biosecurity governance.
